\chapter{KZG Polynomial Commitment}
\label{chap:kzg}

The KZG polynomial commitment scheme was introduced by Kate,
Zaverucha, and Goldberg in 2010 \cite{kate2010}. It is the most
widely deployed polynomial commitment in practice, forming the core
of systems like PLONK, Marlin, and many Ethereum scaling solutions.
The commitment is a single elliptic curve point, the proof is also
a single point, and verification requires exactly two pairings
regardless of the polynomial degree. The cost of this efficiency is
a one-time trusted setup that grows linearly with the degree bound.

\section{KZG Construction}

\textbf{Trusted Setup:}

The setup generates a structured reference string (SRS) from a
secret value $\tau \in \mathbb{F}_r$ (the toxic waste):
\begin{equation}
    \mathsf{srs} = \bigl(
        [1]_1,\; [\tau]_1,\; \ldots,\; [\tau^d]_1,\;
        [1]_2,\; [\tau]_2
    \bigr)
\end{equation}

where $[x]_i$ means the generator of $\mathbb{G}_i$ multiplied by
$x$. Once the SRS is generated, $\tau$ must be discarded. In
practice this is done through a multi-party ceremony.
\\
\textbf{Commitment:}

Given $f(X) = \sum_{i=0}^{d} a_i X^i$, the commitment is:
\begin{equation}
    C = \sum_{i=0}^{d} a_i \cdot [\tau^i]_1 = [f(\tau)]_1
\end{equation}
\\
\textbf{Proof:}
To prove $f(z) = y$, the prover computes the quotient polynomial
$q(X) = (f(X) - y) / (X - z)$ and commits to it:
\begin{equation}
    \pi = [q(\tau)]_1
\end{equation}
\\
\textbf{Verification:}
The verifier checks the following pairing equation:
\begin{equation}
    e\!\left(\pi,\; [\tau]_2 - [z]_2\right)
    \stackrel{?}{=}
    e\!\left(C - [y]_1,\; [1]_2\right)
\end{equation}

This requires exactly two pairings regardless of degree. Security
relies on the $q$-Strong Diffie-Hellman assumption.

\section{Pseudocode}

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{KZG Setup}
\label{alg:kzg-setup}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require $g_1 \in \mathbb{G}_1$, $g_2 \in \mathbb{G}_2$,
         secret $\tau$, degree bound $d$
\Ensure  $\mathsf{crs\_g1}$, $\mathsf{crs\_g2}$, $g_{2,\tau}$
\State $t \gets 1$
\For{$i = 0$ \textbf{to} $d$}
    \State $\mathsf{crs\_g1}.\mathsf{push}(t \cdot g_1)$;\;
           $\mathsf{crs\_g2}.\mathsf{push}(t \cdot g_2)$
    \State $t \gets t \times \tau$
\EndFor
\State $g_{2,\tau} \gets \tau \cdot g_2$
\State \Return $(\mathsf{crs\_g1},\; \mathsf{crs\_g2},\; g_{2,\tau})$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{KZG Commit}
\label{alg:kzg-commit}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require $\mathsf{crs\_g1}$, coefficients $\mathbf{a} = [a_0, \ldots, a_n]$
\Ensure  $C \in \mathbb{G}_1$
\State $C \gets \mathsf{MSM}(\mathsf{crs\_g1}[0..n].\mathsf{to\_affine}(),\; \mathbf{a})$
\State \Return $C$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{KZG Open (Prove)}
\label{alg:kzg-open}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require $\mathsf{crs\_g1}$, coefficients $[a_0, \ldots, a_n]$, point $z$
\Ensure  $\pi \in \mathbb{G}_1$
\State $y \gets \mathsf{evaluate}([a_0, \ldots, a_n],\; z)$
\State $\mathsf{num} \gets [a_0 - y,\; a_1,\; \ldots,\; a_n]$;\;
       $\mathsf{den} \gets [-z,\; 1]$
\State $q \gets \mathsf{poly\_div}(\mathsf{num},\; \mathsf{den})$
\State \Return $\mathsf{Commit}(q)$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{KZG Verify}
\label{alg:kzg-verify}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require $g_1,\; g_2,\; g_{2,\tau}$, $C$, $z$, $y$, $\pi$
\Ensure  \textbf{accept} or \textbf{reject}
\State $\mathsf{lhs} \gets e(\pi,\; g_{2,\tau} - z \cdot g_2)$;\;
       $\mathsf{rhs} \gets e(C - y \cdot g_1,\; g_2)$
\State \Return $\mathsf{lhs} = \mathsf{rhs}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Implementation}

The implementation is written in Rust using the \texttt{arkworks}
library \cite{arkworks}. The \texttt{KZG} struct is generic over
the \texttt{Pairing} trait, so the same code runs on all three
curves without modification. Setup computes powers of $\tau$
iteratively. Commitment and proof generation both use Pippenger's
MSM via \texttt{VariableBaseMSM}, with SRS points converted to
affine before the call. Verification calls \texttt{ark\_ec::pairing}
for the two pairing evaluations. The Pippenger optimisation is
discussed in detail in Chapter~7.
\\\\
\textbf{Source Code:}
The full implementation is available at
\url{https://github.com/farru2610/MTP-II/tree/main/KZG}.
\\\\
\textbf{Benchmark Setup:}

Benchmarks are averaged over 10 runs. Degrees tested:
$d \in \{4, 8, 16, 32, 64, 128, 256, 512, 1024\}$.
Scalar multiplication and pairing costs are measured once per curve
since they are degree-independent.

The proof is always one compressed $\mathbb{G}_1$ point:
\textbf{48 bytes} for BLS12-381 and BLS12-377, \textbf{32 bytes}

\section{Discussion and Summary}

BN254 is the fastest curve on every metric due to its smaller field,
but offers only 100-110 bits of security. BLS12-381 and BLS12-377
both provide 128-bit security; BLS12-381 is slightly faster for
single-proof workloads while BLS12-377 is preferred for recursive
proof composition.

The standout property of KZG is constant verification: at degree
1024, BN254 still verifies in 2.53 ms, the same as at degree 4.
KZG also produces the smallest proofs of all four schemes: 32 or
48 bytes regardless of degree.
